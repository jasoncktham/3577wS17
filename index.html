About

This class meets Tuesdays and Thursdays, 8:15am to 9:30am, at Room 303, Lind Hall.

This course is designed to provide you with:

  • a grounding in elements of rhetorical theories particularly suited to analyzing, critiquing, and understanding the Internet;
  • a sense of the Internet as a historical and cultural phenomenon;
  • an opportunity to examine and understand the technologies underpinning the Internet, and the people developing and implementing these technologies;
  • a clear sense of the Internet as a communicative space, and the likely consequences of our use of this new medium.
(Course objectives adapted from Professor John Logie's syllabus.)

We will discuss ideas about the relationships between rhetoric and technological inventions, and perform analysis of internet communications to learn about ways technology can be rhetoricized and ways rhetoric can be technologized.

Assignments

Texts
All texts can be obtained through the University Bookstore. Alternatively, you may buy new or used versions of these texts from other outlets. Please be sure to get the right edition if you choose not to buy them from the University Bookstore. Except for Eyman’s Digital Rhetoric, all texts below are required by this course:

  • Andrew Blum, Tubes: A Journey to the Center of the Internet
  • Johnny Ryan, A History of the Internet and the Digital Future
  • Richard Toye, Rhetoric: A Very Short Introduction
  • Samuel Greengard, The Internet of Things
  • Gina Neff and Dawn Nafus, Self-Tracking
  • (Optional) Douglas Eyman, Digital Rhetoric: Theory, Method, Practice (free online)

My research is focused on the overlapping areas of rhetoric, technology, and pedagogy. I have a particular interest in wearables and emerging technologies, which involve virtual and augmented realities. Recently, I have written articles on:

  • How Google Glass affects technical communication practices

Policies

Readings for the class are required. Please keep track of the assignments on the syllabus and be prepared to respond to the readings in class.

Attendance is required for all class sessions. The success of this course depends on student presence and participation. Therefore, in this class, after three absences, I may elect to drop your final course grade by one full letter grade for each subsequent absence. Anyone missing seven or more classes will not pass the course. Excessive tardiness may be treated as absenteeism. If you do miss class, it is your responsibility to secure the assignments (preferably from your classmates) and complete the work.

Classroom Decorum 1: Please restrict your use of screens to class-related activities.

Classroom Decorum 2: Please respect all students’ contribution in this class. This doesn’t mean that you have to agree with every opinion from your peers, but you are expected to listen with respect and always provide support to claims you choose to make.

Communications: There are two ways to get a hold of me––email and office hours. Please include “3577” in your email subject line if you would like me to get back to you at a reasonable response speed. All office conferences must be scheduled at least 24 hours in advance. Think of my office as an extension of the classroom, and use my office hours to discuss any aspect of your reading or writing, problems, topics, papers you're working on, ideas you wish to develop, strategies you'd like to try. Plan on having at least one in-person conference with me this semester to discuss your work and your progress in the course.

Academic Dishonesty: Always give credit where it’s due. Be cautious whenever you wish to make use of others’ materials and make sure to clearly and effectively cite your sources. If you are uncertain as to how to document or acknowledge your dependencies, please contact me before you formally submit the project in question and I will be happy to address your questions and concerns.

Students with Disabilities: Appropriate accommodations will be made for all students with documented disabilities. If you have a disability requiring accommodation in this class, please notify me as soon as possible. This information will be kept confidential.

Archiving Your Work: You will need to maintain an electronic archive of all of your work for this class. I may ask to review your work at some point in the semester..
